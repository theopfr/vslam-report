vSLAM

An interactive guide to Visual Simultaneous Location And Mapping

Author

Theodor Peifer, Amelie Hora, Japheth Legesse
Munich University of Applies Sciences

Abstract

Visual SLAM (Simultaneous Localization and Mapping) is a computer vision technique that allows a camera to estimate its motion while simultaneously building a 3D map of an unknown environment. It works by detecting and tracking visual features across images, estimating camera movement between frames and reconstructing scene structure from multiple views. VSLAM is widely used in robotics and autonomous navigation and relies only on a camera instead of depth sensors.

Pipeline

1. Feature Detection

Feature detection finds distinctive, repeatable points in an image, corners and blobs that can be re-identified across frames. Below you can run an ORB feature detector live on your own camera feed:

How it works

The demo above runs ORB (Oriented FAST and Rotated BRIEF), a fast combination of a corner detector (FAST) and a binary descriptor (BRIEF). This feature detection method consists of four distinct steps, as shown below:


  1. FAST Corner test: Look at a ring of 16 pixels around a candidate pixel \(p\). Each ring pixel of intensity \(I_x\) is classified against the centre intensity \(I_p\) and a threshold \(t\): \[ S_x = \begin{cases} \text{brighter} & \text{if } I_x \geq I_p + t \\ \text{similar} & \text{if } I_p - t < I_x < I_p + t \\ \text{darker} & \text{if } I_x \leq I_p - t \end{cases} \] where \(I_p\) is the candidate’s intensity and \(t\) a fixed threshold (the FAST THRESHOLD slider). \(p\) is a corner if \(\geq 9\) contiguous ring pixels are all brighter or all darker. Survivors are ranked by Harris corner score and the best \(N\) (the MAX FEATURES slider) are kept.
  1. Corner Orientation: Each corner is assigned an angle using the intensity centroid method, which points from the patch centre toward its brightest side (the orientation line in the demo’s Rich keypoints view). Steering the descriptor by this angle \(\theta\) is what makes ORB rotation-invariant.
  2. BRIEF Descriptor: Take the \(31\times31\) patch centred on the corner. A fixed set of 256 pixel pairs \((a_i,b_i)\) - read from a precomputed lookup table, already rotated to the corner’s angle \(\theta\) so the result is rotation-invariant - is sampled inside this patch. Each pair yields one bit by comparing intensity: \(\tau_i = 1\) if \(I(a_i) < I(b_i)\), else \(0\). Concatenating all 256 gives a 256-bit (32-byte) descriptor. Later, descriptors will be compared across frames to find correspondences - the same scene point seen in two images.
  3. Scale pyramid: Steps 1–3 run on each level of a pyramid of \(N\) (the PYRAMID LEVELS slider) progressively downscaled images (ratio = PYRAMID SCALE FACTOR slider). A feature is detected at whatever level it is sharpest, giving scale invariance - the fixed \(31\times31\) patch covers more of the scene on coarser levels. The demo otherwise draws just a dot per keypoint; the descriptors feed the matching step next.

2. Feature Matching

Once features are extracted, they have to be matched across frames - finding the same real-world point seen from two different camera positions. Below, the left panel is your live camera and the right panel is a frame from a moment ago; lines link the keypoints ORB matched between them. Move the camera slowly to see the correspondences shift.

How it works

Each keypoint carries the 256-bit descriptor from the previous step. Matching asks: for a descriptor in frame \(F_t\), which descriptor in the earlier frame \(F_{t-1}\) is most similar? Similarity is the Hamming distance - the number of differing bits (a fast XOR + bit-count) - and each descriptor is paired with its nearest neighbour. A few cheap filters (a distance threshold, a cross-check) throw out the unreliable pairs, leaving a set of probable correspondences. The next step, Camera Motion Estimation, makes the final call on which ones are geometrically consistent.

To see what a descriptor actually responds to, the two images below each hold a corner plus a fixed circle in the top-left. Drag the corner to move it (inside the dashed circle), or outside to rotate. The dashed circle is the descriptor patch - the only region that’s compared. Because the descriptor is centred on the corner and rotation-invariant, it keeps matching as you move and rotate it - until you drag it near the circle: once that intrudes on the patch, the bits flip and the match breaks.

3. Camera Motion Estimation

Now that we have matched features, we can work out how the camera moved between two frames. First, one requirement.

The camera has to be calibrated

To recover real motion we need a calibrated camera - one whose internal optics we’ve measured in advance. Calibration comes down to two things, gathered into the intrinsic matrix K:

  • the focal length \(f\) - how strongly the lens magnifies, i.e. how a pixel offset relates to an angle in the scene;
  • the principal point \((c_x, c_y)\) - where the optical axis actually pierces the image (usually near the centre).

\[ K = \begin{bmatrix} f & 0 & c_x \\ 0 & f & c_y \\ 0 & 0 & 1 \end{bmatrix} \]

With K known, a pixel can be turned back into a real-world direction - and that’s what lets us recover true motion instead of a distorted version of it.

What we’re looking for

Between frame A (\(F_{t-1}\)) and frame B (\(F_t\)) the camera has moved, and that motion is just two things:

  • a rotation R - how the camera turned;
  • a translation t - which way it shifted.

The scene below shows exactly that: the same camera at two positions, A and B. Drag to orbit, and move B with the sliders - the white arrow is t and the white arc is R, the two quantities we’re after. The × on each image plane marks where the same feature point lands in each view - that’s a matched pair from the previous step.

Packing R and t into the essential matrix E

R and t are usually bundled into one 3×3 matrix, the essential matrix E:

\[ E = [\,t\,]_\times \, R \]

where R is the rotation and \([\,t\,]_\times\) is the translation \(t\) written as a matrix:

\[ [\,t\,]_\times = \begin{bmatrix} 0 & -t_z & t_y \\ t_z & 0 & -t_x \\ -t_y & t_x & 0 \end{bmatrix} \]

So E is just a compact way of writing where B sits relative to A. We never measure R and t directly - all we have are the ×’s, the pixel where each camera sees the same feature. A match gives a point \(x\) in frame A and its partner \(x'\) in frame B, and every correct match obeys one simple relation:

\[ x'^{\top}\, E \, x = 0 \]

Each match gives one such equation, so with about eight matches there are enough to solve for E. Once E is known, it splits back into R and t - the motion we were after, which we chain across frames to track the camera and hand to triangulation for the 3D map.

One catch: from a single camera the length of \(t\) can’t be recovered, only its direction - so the trajectory and map come out correct in shape but at an arbitrary overall scale.

4. Triangulation

We now know where the camera was for each frame (its R and t) and which pixels in the two views are the same feature (the matched ×’s). That’s everything we need to place the feature in 3D - by triangulation.

The idea is simple. A pixel doesn’t give a 3D point, only a ray: back-project it from the camera centre through the pixel and the true point lies somewhere along that ray, at unknown depth. But the same feature seen from the second camera gives a second ray - and two rays from different viewpoints cross at a single point in space. That intersection is the feature’s 3D position.

In the scene below, each camera back-projects its matched pixel into a ray (green from A, amber from B); where they meet is the 3D point. Drag to orbit.

Two things the demo makes clear:

  1. Baseline matters. With the cameras far apart the rays cross at a wide, well-defined angle. Shrink the baseline and they become nearly parallel, so the intersection gets shallow and the depth poorly constrained - the reason distant points are always the most uncertain.
  2. Real matches are noisy. A pixel match is never exact, so the two rays almost never meet perfectly - they pass near each other instead. Add some match error and watch the rays skew apart: triangulation then takes the midpoint of the shortest gap between them (a small least-squares problem), shown as the white estimate next to the true point.

What we need to compute the point

By now every ingredient is already in hand:

  • \(K\) - the \(3\times3\) intrinsic matrix (from calibration);
  • \(R_1, t_1\) and \(R_2, t_2\) - the pose of each camera (from motion estimation);
  • \(x_1 = (u_1, v_1)\) and \(x_2 = (u_2, v_2)\) - the matched pixel in each image (from feature matching).

From the poses and intrinsics, form each camera’s \(3\times4\) projection matrix:

\[ P_1 = K\,[\,R_1 \mid t_1\,], \qquad P_2 = K\,[\,R_2 \mid t_2\,] \]

Each view ties the unknown 3D point \(X\) to its pixel by \(x \simeq P\,X\). Writing \(P^{(i)}\) for the \(i\)-th row of a projection matrix, the two views stack into a single \(4\times4\) system:

\[ A = \begin{bmatrix} u_1\,P_1^{(3)} - P_1^{(1)} \\[2pt] v_1\,P_1^{(3)} - P_1^{(2)} \\[2pt] u_2\,P_2^{(3)} - P_2^{(1)} \\[2pt] v_2\,P_2^{(3)} - P_2^{(2)} \end{bmatrix}, \qquad A\,X = 0 \]

Solving \(A\,X = 0\) with an SVD - taking the smallest singular vector - gives the 3D point \(X\). This is the Direct Linear Transform (DLT), and the SVD’s least-squares nature is exactly the demo’s behaviour: when the rays don’t quite meet, \(X\) lands at the best compromise between them.

Doing this for every matched feature turns the set of 2D correspondences into a set of 3D points - the beginnings of the map, which the final step assembles into a point cloud.

5. Sparse Point Cloud Map

Triangulation gives 3D points from one pair of frames. A real sequence has hundreds of frames, and SLAM simply keeps doing this: for every new frame it estimates the camera’s pose, triangulates the features it shares with earlier frames, and drops the new 3D points into one shared world coordinate frame. The accumulated result is the map - together with the chain of poses, which is the camera’s trajectory.
